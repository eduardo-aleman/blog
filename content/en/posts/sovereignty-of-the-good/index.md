Iris Murdoch held a singular perspective on metaphor, which she explored at length in her philosophical writings. In *Metaphysics as a Guide to Morals*, she delves into the role of metaphorical thinking in shaping our understanding of reality and our moral lives.

Murdoch argued that metaphors are not mere linguistic devices, but essential instruments through which we perceive and make sense of the world. Far from being decorative or ornamental, they can profoundly shape our thinking and influence the way we relate to reality.

According to Murdoch, metaphors play a crucial role in moral perception and understanding: they illuminate aspects of our experience that are difficult to express directly, and help us grasp complex ethical concepts and relationships. They also function as tools of the moral imagination, allowing us to conceive of alternative ways of being and acting in the world.

In her essay "The Sovereignty of Good Over Other Concepts," Murdoch explores the good as an indispensable metaphor for moral thought, arguing against the prevailing notion that it can be reduced to a set of rules or principles, and advocating instead for a more nuanced and complex understanding of ethical conduct.

Central to her argument is the idea that the good arises from a deep engagement with reality and a genuine moral attention to the needs and interests of others. She rejects the idea of the good as mere abstraction or ideal, emphasizing instead its practical implications for human conduct and human relationships.

Murdoch also criticizes the tendency to conceptualize morality in terms of rigid moral codes or ethical systems, maintaining that such approaches fail to capture the complexity and richness of moral life, and lead to ethical blindness and moral distortion.

In their place, she proposes that the good involves a kind of moral perception that allows us to see the world and our place in it with greater clarity — one that calls upon empathy, imagination, and the capacity to contemplate things from multiple perspectives.

The following is an excerpt from her essay:

> "I assume that human beings are naturally selfish and that human life has no external point or τέλος. That human beings are naturally selfish seems to be shown by the evidence, whenever and wherever we observe them, in spite of a very small number of apparent exceptions. About the nature of this selfishness modern psychology has something to tell us. The psyche is a historically determined individual relentlessly looking after itself. In some ways it resembles a machine; in order to operate it needs sources of energy, and it is predisposed to certain patterns of activity. The area of its much vaunted freedom of choice is not normally very great. One of its chief pastimes is day-dreaming. It is reluctant to face unpleasant realities. Its consciousness is not normally a transparent crystal through which it views the world, but a cloud of more or less fantastic reverie designed to protect the psyche from pain. It constantly seeks consolation, either through imaginary inflation of the self or through consolations of a theological nature. Even its love is, too often, an assertion of self. I think we can probably recognise ourselves in this rather depressing description."

Another excerpt:

> "I have been speaking of the indefinability of Good; but is there really nothing more we can say about it? Even if we cannot find another name for it, even if it must be thought of as solitary and lofty, are there not other concepts, or some other concept, with which it has some very special relation? Philosophers have often tried to discern such a relation: Freedom, Reason, Happiness, Courage, History have recently been tried for the purpose. I do not find any of these candidates convincing. They seem in each case to represent the philosopher's admiration for some specific aspect of human conduct considerably below total excellence and sometimes dubious in itself. I have already mentioned one concept with a certain claim and I will return to it in conclusion. I want now to speak of what is perhaps the most obvious as well as the oldest and most traditional candidate, though one that is rarely mentioned by our contemporary philosophers, and that is Love. Of course Good is sovereign over Love, as it is sovereign over other concepts, because love can name something bad [...]"

Finally:

> "Perhaps the finding of other names for Good or the establishing of its special relations is just a sort of game. However I want, as a conclusion, to make just one more move. Goodness is connected with the acceptance of real death and real chance and real transience, and only in the context of this acceptance, which is psychologically so difficult, can we understand the full extent of what virtue is. The acceptance of death is an acceptance of our own nothingness, which is an automatic spur to concern ourselves with what is not ourselves. The good man is humble; he is very unlike the neo-Kantian great Lucifer. He is much more like the Kierkegaard tax-collector. Humility is a rare virtue and an unfashionable one, and one that is often hard to discern. Only rarely does one come across somebody in whom it genuinely shines, in whom one perceives with wonder the absence of the anxious avaricious tentacles of the self. In fact any other name for Good must be a partial name; but the names of virtues suggest directions of thought, and this direction seems to me a better one than that suggested by more popular conceptions such as freedom and courage. The humble man, because he sees himself as nothing, can see other things as they are. He sees the pointlessness of virtue as well as its unique value and the endless extent of its demand. Simone Weil tells us that the exposure of the soul to God condemns its selfish part not to suffering but to death. The humble man perceives the distance between suffering and death. And although he is not by definition the good man, perhaps he is the kind of man who is most likely to become good."
