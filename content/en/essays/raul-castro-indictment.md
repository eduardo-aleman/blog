The federal grand jury in Miami returned the indictment in April. The Justice Department unsealed it on May 20 — the anniversary of the day in 1902 when the United States formally ended its military occupation of Cuba. The press conference was held at the Freedom Tower, the building where Cubans fleeing the revolution were once processed for asylum. South Florida mayors and local politicians sat in the audience. The acting Attorney General announced that Raúl Castro, age 94, was wanted on charges of murder and conspiracy to kill American citizens, and that he expected the defendant to appear in a US courtroom "by his own will or another way."

Two things are worth holding in mind at once about this announcement. It is a genuine symbolic blow to the Cuban regime. And it is wrapped in stagecraft so elaborate that the legal substance is almost incidental to the political function. Reading the difference between those two layers matters more than the headlines suggest.

## The symbolic weight

Start with the symbolism, because it is real. No US administration has ever criminally indicted a sitting or former Cuban head of state for acts undertaken as head of state. The 2003 indictment of one of the Cuban pilots involved in the 1996 Brothers to the Rescue shootdown was an outlier — a charge against a uniformed officer for a specific operational act. Bringing the same case up the chain of command to the man who, with his brother, made the final decision is a categorically different gesture. It treats the Cuban revolution not as an adversary government with whose policies Washington disagrees, but as a criminal enterprise whose senior leadership is personally liable under American law.

Raúl Castro is the obvious target. After Fidel's death in 2016, he became the living embodiment of the Revolution. He is not the formal head of state — Miguel Díaz-Canel holds that role — but he remains the most powerful figure in Cuba, and within the country's iconography he is the surviving founder. To charge him with murder is to charge the Revolution itself in legal form. That is the gesture the Cuban government recognized in its response, which framed the indictment as a "canalla" attack against "the Líder of the Revolution." They are not pretending he is a private citizen, and Washington is not pretending it has charged one.

The legal framing does additional work. It collapses a Cuban state decision — the response to repeated unauthorized overflights of national territory by a Miami-based exile organization — into ordinary murder of Americans. Whatever one's view of the merits of the 1996 shoot-down (the underlying facts remain contested, with declassified US documents partially supporting the Cuban claim that Washington was warned in advance and chose not to act), the prosecution erases any distinction between state action and crime. That is precisely the framing required to use the US criminal justice system as a regime-change tool, as the Maduro precedent has now established.

## The stagecraft

Then there is the theater, and it is hard to overstate how thick it is. The indictment was returned a month before it was announced. The grand jury did not choose May 20 — the Justice Department did. The Freedom Tower is not a federal courthouse. South Florida mayors do not normally attend federal indictment announcements. The acting Attorney General's line about Castro appearing "by his own will or another way" is not language a prosecutor uses about a defendant; it is language a head of state uses about an enemy. Across town, the Secretary of State released a Spanish-language video addressed directly to the Cuban people. Within hours, the aircraft carrier *Nimitz* entered the southern Caribbean. All of these things happened in the same news cycle, and none of them happened by accident.

The legal action and the political performance are not in tension, exactly. They are doing different work. The indictment supplies a legal scaffolding that can later be invoked to justify almost anything from continued sanctions to a military raid. The performance — the Freedom Tower, the date, the carrier, the Rubio video, the language from the podium — is for two audiences. The first is Cuban-American voters in South Florida, whose political weight has driven the case forward for three decades and whose February letter to the President helped prompt this round of charges. The second is the Cuban public itself, who are being told as bluntly as possible that the United States considers the people running their country to be murderers. The choice of May 20 sharpens that second message. As one historian put it in the coverage, the administration is "hearkening back to this moment when the US did treat Cuba as its backyard."

This is the unusual feature of the announcement that gives away its character. A prosecution that was about justice for the four men killed in 1996 would not require an aircraft carrier, a parallel address from the Secretary of State, or a venue chosen for its emotional resonance with the Cuban-American diaspora. Those are not prosecutorial choices. They are the choreography of pressure.

## Does a raid follow?

Which raises the question of whether a Venezuela-style operation actually comes next.

My estimate is that the probability of a Maduro-style commando raid in Cuba over the next six to twelve months sits at roughly 15 to 25 percent. The full apparatus is in place — indictment, carrier, public-facing rationale — and the January operation in Caracas is fresh enough that ruling out a sequel would be foolish. But several factors cut the other way.

The most important is that Trump himself told reporters on Wednesday, "No, there won't be escalation. I don't think there needs to be," adding that Cuba is "falling apart." That sentence almost certainly reflects an internal assessment that the oil blockade — which has produced 22-hour blackouts and black-market gasoline above 40 dollars a gallon — is doing the regime-change work without the political cost of military action. The *Nimitz* is reportedly there to signal, not to launch operations, an explicit contrast with how the *Gerald Ford* was used in January. And the operational case for a raid is materially weaker than Venezuela's: Raúl Castro is 94 and almost certainly not making day-to-day decisions, Cuba is an island with a more cohesive single-party security apparatus, and a Caracas-style snatch-and-extract is a categorically harder military problem in Havana than it was in a fractured Bolivarian state.

The likelier path is sustained economic strangulation aimed at producing either internal collapse, a negotiated transition that sidelines GAESA — the military conglomerate Rubio named in his address — or both. The CIA director's quiet visit to Havana last week, in which he reportedly demanded economic restructuring and the eviction of Russian and Chinese intelligence operations, suggests Washington is testing what Havana will concede before force becomes necessary. Cuba's ambassador to the UN gave his first on-the-record interview in years specifically to signal that Havana is open to talks. That is the behavior of a government that knows the squeeze is working, even as it publicly denounces the squeeze as illegitimate.

## What the indictment actually is

Both readings are correct, and they are not the same reading. The indictment is a real symbolic blow — it places the United States in a new posture toward Cuba's leadership, in which the regime is not just opposed but criminalized, and it gives South Florida a long-deferred satisfaction that previous administrations declined to provide. And the indictment is a piece of political stagecraft, scheduled and staged for maximum domestic and hemispheric resonance, that does not by itself tell us much about whether a raid is coming.

The most likely outcome, in my view, is that the indictment never produces a defendant in a US courtroom, that Raúl Castro dies in Havana, and that the legal scaffolding put in place this week becomes one more piece of leverage in a slower, grinding campaign of economic and diplomatic pressure designed to outlast him. Whether that campaign succeeds is a different question, and the Cubans, as their ambassador noted, do not lack practice in surviving it.

The piece worth watching is not the indictment itself. It is whether the talks Ratcliffe opened in Havana produce anything concrete in the next several weeks. If they do, the indictment will have served its purpose as leverage. If they do not, the same scaffolding is still there, waiting.
