America's maximum pressure campaign against Cuba has achieved something remarkable: it has made the island's misery someone else's problem. Cuban migrants who once streamed toward the U.S. border---down 99 percent from the Biden years---now move south instead, flooding Brazil, Mexico, and Uruguay with asylum claims. Brazil alone absorbed more than 41,000 Cuban applicants in 2025, nearly double the previous year; Cubans now make up 55 percent of all asylum-seekers there. Mexico issued humanitarian visitor cards to Cubans at a rate that jumped from 23 percent of its total in 2024 to 78 percent this year. Washington has redirected the pressure without relieving it, and the bill is landing in capitals that have little reason to be grateful.

On the island itself, the arithmetic of survival has become surreal. Cuba has not received a drop of oil in five months. A liter of gasoline---once $1.20---now fetches $8 on the street and $40 a gallon on the black market, on an island where the average monthly wage is $16. Hospitals are postponing surgeries. Families cook over charcoal and wood. China sent humanitarian aid; a Russian tanker carrying 242,000 barrels of diesel turned back midroute rather than risk U.S. sanctions. The blockade is working, in the narrow sense that it is inflicting genuine pain. Whether pain produces the desired political outcome is a different question entirely.

The military dimension has grown harder to ignore. The USS Nimitz now sits in the Caribbean. A federal grand jury has indicted former President Raúl Castro for the 1996 shootdown of civilian aircraft---a legal maneuver that establishes criminal pretext without requiring proof. Analysts agree that Cuba's air defenses, running on Soviet-era hardware and six to eight operational aircraft, could not stop a U.S. strike. The Pentagon's working assumption appears to be that toppling the leadership is "another day at the office." What comes after is the nightmare. Cuba's defense doctrine---*Guerra de Todo el Pueblo*, War of All the People---is explicitly designed to survive decapitation. Remove the figureheads and you may not get collapse; you may get fracture, irregular resistance, and a humanitarian catastrophe ninety miles from Florida with no obvious exit.

Washington frames all of this as self-defense: Cuba is a failed state harboring Chinese and Russian intelligence infrastructure and, according to leaked assessments, 300 military drones. Havana counters that the U.S. has never produced public evidence of those intelligence posts and that the Castro indictment belongs to a long tradition of manufactured pretexts---reaching back to 19th-century anti-piracy doctrines---deployed to dress up coercion as law. Both narratives cannot be entirely right, but history suggests that legal architecture built to justify intervention tends to outlast the political will to question it.

The maximum pressure campaign has strangled Cuba's economy without breaking its government. It has generated a migration crisis that is now Latin America's to manage, created geopolitical leverage for left-leaning governments with little incentive to cooperate with Washington, and positioned the United States at the edge of a military gamble whose downside risks dwarf its stated objectives. The pressure is maximal. The strategy, so far, is not.
