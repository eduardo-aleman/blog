Iris Murdoch held a singular perspective on metaphors, which she frequently explored in her philosophical writings. In _Metaphysics as a Guide to Morals_, a collection of her most prominent essays, she delves into the role of metaphorical thinking in shaping our understanding of reality and our moral life.

Murdoch maintained that metaphors are not merely linguistic devices, but essential instruments with which we perceive and make sense of the world. Far from being decorative or ornamental, they can profoundly shape our thinking and influence the way we relate to reality.

According to Murdoch, metaphors play a crucial role in moral perception and understanding: they illuminate aspects of our experience that are difficult to express directly and help us grasp complex ethical concepts and relations. They also function as tools of moral imagination, allowing us to conceive of alternative ways of being and acting in the world.

## The Sovereignty of the Good

"The Sovereignty of the Good over other concepts" is the finest essay in the collection. There, Murdoch explores the good as an indispensable metaphor of moral thought, and argues against the prevailing notion that morality can be reduced to a set of rules or principles, advocating instead for a more nuanced and complex understanding of ethical conduct.

A central element of her argument is the idea that the good arises from a profound commitment to reality and genuine moral attention to the needs and interests of others. Murdoch rejects the idea of the good as mere abstraction or ideal, instead emphasizing its practical implications for conduct and human relations.

## Critique of Rigid Ethical Systems

Murdoch also criticizes the tendency to conceptualize morality in terms of rigid codes or ethical systems, arguing that such approaches fail to capture the complexity and richness of moral life, and lead to moral blindness and moral distortion.

Instead, she proposes that the good involves a kind of moral perception that allows us to see the world and our place in it with greater clarity. To this end, she emphasizes the importance of empathy, imagination, and the capacity to contemplate things from multiple perspectives.

## Fragments from the Essay

> I assume that human beings are naturally selfish and that human life has no external point or τέλος. That human beings are naturally selfish seems to be shown by the evidence, wherever and whenever we observe them, despite a very small number of apparent exceptions. Concerning the nature of this selfishness, modern psychology has something to tell us. The psyche is a historically determined individual who is constantly seeking itself. In some respects it resembles a machine; to operate it needs sources of energy, and it is predisposed to certain patterns of activity. The area of its vaunted freedom of choice is normally not very large. One of its greatest pastimes is daydreaming. It is reluctant to face unpleasant realities. Its consciousness is not normally a transparent crystal through which it looks at the world, but rather a cloud of more or less fantastic dreams designed to protect the psyche from pain. It is constantly seeking comfort, either through an imaginary inflation of the self or through fictions of a theological nature. Even its love is, too often, an affirmation of the self. I think we can probably recognize ourselves in this depressing description.

> I have been talking about the indefinability of Good; but is there really nothing more that we can say about it? Even if we cannot find another name for it, even if it must be thought as solitary and elevated, are there not other concepts, or another concept, with which it has some very special relation? Philosophers have often tried to discern such a relation: Freedom, Reason, Happiness, Courage or History have recently been tried for this purpose. I find none of these candidates convincing. They seem to represent in each case the philosopher's admiration for some specific aspect of human conduct far below total excellence and, sometimes, questionable in itself. I have already mentioned one concept with a certain claim and in the conclusion I shall return to it. I want to speak now of what is perhaps the most obvious, as well as the most ancient and traditional candidate, though rarely mentioned by our contemporary philosophers, and that is Love. Of course the Good is sovereign over Love, as it is sovereign over the other concepts, because love can name something evil [...]

---

> Perhaps the finding of other names for the Good or the establishment of its special relations are no more than a kind of personal game. However, I want, in conclusion, to make just one more move. Goodness is related to the acceptance of real death, real chance and real transience, and only in the context of this acceptance—psychologically so difficult—can we understand in all its fullness what virtue is. The acceptance of death is an acceptance of our own nothingness, which is an automatic incentive that we should concern ourselves with what is not ourselves. The good man is humble; he is very different from the great neo-Kantian Lucifer. He is much more like Kierkegaard's tax-collector. Humility is a rare virtue, old-fashioned and often difficult to discern. One rarely encounters someone in whom it shines authentically, in whom one perceives with wonder the absence of the anxious and avaricious tentacles of the self. Indeed, any other name for the Good must be a partial name; but the names of the virtues suggest directions of thought, and this direction seems to me better than that suggested by more popular conceptions such as freedom and courage. The humble man, because he sees himself as nothing, can see other things as they are. He sees the pointlessness of virtue, its unique value and the endless reach of its demands. Simone Weil tells us that the exposure of the soul to God condemns its selfish part not to suffering but to death. The humble man perceives the distance between suffering and death. And although he is not by definition the good man, perhaps he is the kind of man who is most likely to become good.
